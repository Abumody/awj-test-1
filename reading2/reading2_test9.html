Mission to Mars Application

11th January 2014

Dear Mr Lansdorp,

When a friend told me about your plan to set up a permanent colony on Mars, I thought it was a crazy idea — especially when he told me that the colonists would never be able to return to Earth! But then I saw an interview with you on TV, and your obvious enthusiasm for the project changed my mind completely. And when I heard you talk about the characteristics that you were looking for in potential colonists, I realised that I was a very suitable candidate — so I decided to apply!

Let me tell you something about myself. I work for the University of Toronto in Canada. I have two qualifications that are essential for anyone who wishes to start life in space — a Master’s degree in Biology and a PhD in Ecology. I have worked on several international projects in places as varied as China, Mexico, Egypt and Algeria. At the moment, I'm in Antarctica working with a research group on an agricultural experiment. Because of the extreme conditions found here — especially the freezing temperatures — there are obvious similarities with Mars. Even though the soil may be different, if we succeed in growing plants here, I'm sure that we can do the same on Mars. This kind of knowledge will, I believe, be crucial for the success of the mission.

Another important characteristic that was mentioned in your interview is the ability to deal with stressful situations. I think I have proved that I can do this. For example, two months ago, a fire broke out in our lab, caused by an electrical fault. I didn’t panic, and was able to rescue two injured colleagues and also to control the fire before it damaged any important equipment.

You also mentioned the psychological strength needed for a one-way mission to Mars. Colonists will have to deal with the certainty that they will never see Earth or their loved ones again. However, I believe that I have learned to cope with this kind of isolation. Here in Antarctica, I see the same three faces every day — just as I would do on Mars — and we all get on very well together. In addition, I have no children, and five years ago, I lost both my parents in a car accident and my wife after a long battle with leukemia.

Finally, I have one question. Since watching your interview, I have looked closely at your website, and I notice that it doesn’t mention any kind of age-limit. What it says is that, after selection, there will be six years of training, starting later this year. I ask because, although I’m in excellent health, I am now thirty-five — which means that I shall be forty-one when the journey to Mars takes place. Will I still be considered?

Yours,
Mark Maxim
